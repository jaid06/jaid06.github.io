About Me

My Research Experience

I am a fifth year Ph.D. Candidate in Biochemistry at the University of Houston. I work the Antunes Lab, led by Dr. Dinler Antunes, where I specialize in structural bioinformatics and immunoinformatics to improve immunotherapies and vaccine design.

Using these techniques, my thesis specifically aims to improve cancer vaccine design for Black and Latine patients, which was funded by NIH and NIMHD. This was done with the development of my novel tool, Peptide Point-Mutation Generator (PPmGen), enabling the generation of efficacious and safe peptides for Black and Latine patients with PIK3CA-associated breast cancer. The main goal of my thesis is to provide an innovative immunoinformatics pipeline that can identify safe and efficacious cancer vaccine candidates for all patients, regardless of their demographics and cancer type.

I have also worked in collaboration with other scientists to study the biophysical impacts of immunotherapy drugs targeting the innate immune system, identifying safe cancer vaccine targets for KIF5B-RET associated lung cancer, and identifying peptide candidates for viral vaccines.

Additionally, my experience also involves executing and improving molecular docking, Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA) binding algorithm, and molecular dynamics simulation (MDS) protocols for various peptide-HLA and drug-protein complexes. I am proficient in using the Python, Jupyter Notebook, Bash, and RStudio coding platforms and languages for the sake of analysis, structural modeling, and immunoinformatics.

My future research goals involve using immunoinformatics and wet lab techniques to improve immunotherapy design for different cancer types, Systemic Lupus Erythematosus, and sickle cell anemia.
